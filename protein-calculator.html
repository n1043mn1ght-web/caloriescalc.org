Why protein intake matters

Protein is the only macronutrient that directly builds and repairs muscle tissue. While carbohydrates and fat provide energy, protein provides the amino acid building blocks your body uses to create and maintain muscle, enzymes, hormones, and immune cells.

For body composition — whether your goal is losing fat, gaining muscle, or both — adequate protein intake is the single most important dietary variable. Research consistently shows that higher protein diets produce better body composition outcomes than lower protein diets at the same total calorie intake.

Protein during fat loss

When eating below your TDEE, your body breaks down both fat and muscle for energy. High protein intake signals your body to preferentially burn fat and preserve muscle. Studies show that dieters consuming 2.2–2.4g/kg of protein lose the same amount of weight as those consuming 1.2g/kg — but retain significantly more lean muscle mass.

Key insight: Protein needs are actually higher during a cut than during maintenance. The leaner you get, the more important adequate protein becomes for muscle preservation.

Protein during muscle gain

Muscle protein synthesis is maximised at around 1.6–2.2g/kg/day for most people. Consuming more than ~2.2g/kg provides diminishing returns for muscle building, though it causes no harm in healthy individuals. The effective upper limit appears to be around 3.1g/kg for advanced athletes with very high training volumes.

Protein and satiety

Protein is the most satiating macronutrient — gram for gram, it reduces hunger more than carbohydrates or fat. This makes high-protein diets easier to sustain in a calorie deficit. Additionally, your body burns approximately 20–30% of protein calories during digestion (thermic effect), compared to 5–10% for carbs and 0–3% for fat.

Frequently asked questions

How much protein do I need per day?

For active individuals, research consistently supports 1.6–2.2g/kg of bodyweight per day (0.7–1g per lb). Sedentary adults need approximately 0.8g/kg for basic health. This calculator adjusts the target based on your activity level and goal, using multipliers derived from meta-analyses on resistance-trained individuals.

Should I eat more protein when cutting?

Yes — protein needs are higher during a calorie deficit. When eating below maintenance, your body increases protein breakdown for energy. Consuming higher protein (2.2–2.4g/kg) counteracts this, signalling your body to preserve muscle and burn fat preferentially. This is why the fat loss target in this calculator is set higher than maintenance.

Is it better to spread protein across meals or eat it all at once?

Spreading protein across 3–5 meals (around 0.4g/kg per meal) maximises muscle protein synthesis throughout the day. Each protein-rich meal triggers a "pulse" of synthesis lasting 3–5 hours. After that window, another meal is needed to restimulate the process. For most people, this means 30–50g of protein per meal.

Can you eat too much protein?

For healthy individuals with normal kidney function, intakes up to 3g/kg/day have not been shown to cause harm in research studies lasting up to 2 years. The main practical risk is displacing other important nutrients. People with existing kidney disease should consult a doctor before significantly increasing protein intake.

Does protein timing around workouts matter?

Total daily protein intake matters far more than timing. If you consistently hit your daily target, the exact timing around workouts has a relatively small additional effect. That said, consuming 20–40g of protein within 2 hours post-workout is a practical strategy that most people can follow without disrupting their routine.

Do protein needs change with age?

Yes. Adults over 65 experience "anabolic resistance" — a reduced sensitivity to the muscle-building signal of protein. Older adults need more protein per meal (35–40g) to trigger the same response that younger adults get from 20–25g. For older adults, the recommended range rises to 1.8–2.4g/kg, and resistance training remains essential for maintaining muscle mass.
