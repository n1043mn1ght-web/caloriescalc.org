Quick summary: Avocado contains 160 calories per 100g — higher than most fruits — but 77% of those calories come from monounsaturated oleic acid, the same healthy fat found in olive oil. Half an avocado (68g) provides 109 calories, 10% of daily potassium (more than banana), 24% of folate, and 4.6g of fiber. With only 1.8g of net carbs per 100g, avocado is one of the most keto-friendly and diabetes-friendly foods available.

Avocado Health Benefits & Nutrition

Monounsaturated Fat — the Healthy Fat in Avocado

Avocado contains 14.7g of fat per 100g, but 71% of this is oleic acid — the same monounsaturated fat that makes olive oil health-promoting. A landmark 2015 study in the Journal of the American Heart Association found that eating one avocado daily reduced LDL cholesterol by 13.5 mg/dL compared to a low-fat diet — and specifically improved LDL particle size from small (dangerous) to large (benign). This was the first randomised trial to demonstrate avocado's cardiovascular benefits.

More Potassium Than Banana

Avocado contains 485mg of potassium per 100g — 35% more than banana (358mg/100g). A whole avocado provides 975mg of potassium — 21% of the daily value. Adequate potassium intake is one of the most consistently supported dietary factors for blood pressure reduction. The Dietary Approaches to Stop Hypertension (DASH) diet is built partly around potassium-rich foods like avocado.

Fiber — the Highest of Any Common Fruit

Avocado provides 6.7g of fiber per 100g — by far the highest of any commonly eaten fruit. This includes both soluble fiber (which feeds beneficial gut bacteria and lowers cholesterol) and insoluble fiber (which promotes bowel regularity). A half avocado provides 4.6g of fiber — 16% of the daily requirement — in a single serving.

Avocado and Weight Management

Despite being calorie-dense at 160 kcal/100g, avocados are consistently associated with better weight management in population studies. A 2013 study in Nutrition Journal found that people who ate avocado reported 23% greater meal satisfaction and had a 40% reduced desire to eat over 5 hours compared to those who didn't. The combination of fat, fiber, and low net carbs creates powerful satiety per calorie consumed.

Frequently Asked Questions

How many calories in an avocado?
Avocado contains 160 calories per 100g. Half a medium avocado (68g) has 109 calories. A whole medium avocado (146g) has about 234 calories. Use the slider above to calculate for any weight.
Is avocado good for weight loss?
Yes, in appropriate portions. Despite being calorie-dense, avocado's fat and fiber create strong satiety. Studies show people who eat avocado report greater fullness and consume fewer calories at subsequent meals. The key is portion control — half an avocado is a standard serving.
Is avocado keto?
Yes — avocado is one of the best keto foods. It contains only 1.8g of net carbs per 100g, with 14.7g of healthy fat. It's a staple in ketogenic diets for its fat content, nutrient density, and extremely low carbohydrate content.
Does avocado have more potassium than banana?
Yes. Avocado contains 485mg of potassium per 100g, compared to 358mg in banana. A whole avocado provides 975mg of potassium — 21% of the daily value — making it one of the best dietary sources of this mineral.
Is avocado a fruit or vegetable?
Botanically, avocado is a fruit — specifically a large berry with a single seed. It grows on trees and develops from the flower of the plant. Culinary usage varies: avocado is used as both a vegetable (in salads, sandwiches) and a fruit (in smoothies, desserts).
How much fat is in avocado?
A whole medium avocado contains approximately 21g of total fat. Of this, 15g (71%) is monounsaturated fat (oleic acid), 3g (14%) is polyunsaturated fat, and 3g (14%) is saturated fat. The fat profile is virtually identical to olive oil, which is widely considered one of the healthiest dietary fats.
How do I know when avocado is ripe?
A ripe avocado yields to gentle pressure without feeling mushy. The skin darkens from green to nearly black as it ripens (for Hass variety). Remove the stem nub — if green underneath, it's ripe; if brown, it's overripe; if difficult to remove, it's underripe. Ripen avocados at room temperature, then refrigerate to slow further ripening.
